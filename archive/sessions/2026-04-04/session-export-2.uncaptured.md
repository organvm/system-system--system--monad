  ★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────
  The deeper issue: the SOP was designed for archival decomposition of a fixed
  corpus. Applying it to a generative research workspace where the content
  defines its own classification axes creates a self-referential loop — the
  Layer Stratification document both establishes the vocabulary used to classify
   atoms and is itself classified by that vocabulary. This isn't a bug in the
  execution; it's a structural property of applying content-analysis tools to
  self-describing ontological systems. The SOP's implicit assumption — that
  taxonomy is external to content — breaks when the content IS the taxonomy.

  A second-pass protocol might: (a) distinguish meta-atoms (atoms that define
  classification axes) from object-atoms (atoms classified by those axes), (b)
  assign meta-atoms a layers: [meta] tag that explicitly marks self-reference,
  and (c) require that integration priority for meta-atoms always be 1A
  regardless of formalization level, since they are load-bearing for every other
   classification.
